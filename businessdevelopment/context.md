## Part 1: What You're Selling — Hire90 (Ownership Hiring)

### The One Line

Hire the person who'll own your next growth KPI, using a process built for ownership roles where traditional hiring keeps failing.

### The Problem You're Naming

Every growing company has had this experience:

- They need someone to own a number — sales pipeline, conversion, production efficiency, client retention, quality, margins.
- They hire someone who interviews well. Strong resume. Talks a good game.
- The person starts. First month is fine. By month two, they're waiting for direction. The MD expected ownership. The hire expected a playbook.
- Nobody had defined what the role actually demands, day by day. The JD was copy-pasted from Naukri. The interview tested presentation, not whether the person can figure things out on their own.
- 3-6 months later, the MD is back to doing it themselves. ₹5-15 lakh gone. Time wasted. The role is still empty.

This cycle repeats because the standard hiring process — JD, sourcing, interviews — was built for **defined roles** where the job is clear and someone just needs to fill it. **Ownership roles** are different. The role often doesn't exist yet. Nobody can write the JD from a template. And interviews reward people who present well, not people who take ownership.

The MD keeps running the same process for a fundamentally different kind of hire, and keeps getting the same result.

### What Hire90 Does

Three things:

1. **Defines the role against the KPI.** Not a vague JD — daily activities, what success looks like at day 30/60/90, and the persona traits that predict success in this specific role.

2. **Tests candidates through work simulation.** Candidates work on real scenarios from the business before they're hired. "Do the job before you get the job." Instead of asking "tell me about a time when you showed leadership," you watch them actually lead on a real problem.

3. **90-day tracker.** Shared between the employer and the new hire from day one. Makes ownership concrete and measurable. Both sides know what's expected. No surprises at the 3-month review. No "I thought you meant..." conversations.

### Proof

- **AdTech company (50 billion+ impressions):** Freshers became client-ready in 5 weeks and matched 3-year lateral hires within 6 months. The company reused the role blueprint for 10+ subsequent hires and now runs it independently.
- **B2B services company:** The BDR role had never existed in the org. The hire built pipeline from scratch and was productive in month one. They hired a second BDR at half the cycle time six months later.

### What Hire90 is NOT

| Hire90 is NOT... | What it actually is |
|---|---|
| A recruitment agency | We don't source CVs or send profiles. We design the role, build the selection simulation, and structure the 90-day onboarding. |
| An HR tool | Hire90 is for the decision-maker's hire — the MD who personally cares about this role working out. HR handles bulk hiring and defined roles. |
| A job portal | We don't list jobs. We don't have a candidate database. |
| A staffing company | We don't place temps. We build the process that makes a permanent hire succeed. |

If the MD thinks you're any of these, you've lost the framing. Correct it early.

### Language That Works

MDs don't speak in corporate jargon. They describe their problems in plain language. Match that.

| Say this | Not this |
|----------|----------|
| "Ownership hiring" | "Talent acquisition solution" |
| "Someone who owns the number" | "A high-calibre resource" |
| "The hire waited for instructions" | "There was a role-fit misalignment" |
| "Do the job before you get the job" | "Simulation-based assessment methodology" |
| "The 90 days keep everyone honest" | "Structured onboarding framework" |
| "This is your hire, not HR's" | "We collaborate with key stakeholders" |
| "You've been here before" | "Organizations often face this challenge" |

**The word to plant: Ownership.** Every MD in India has said "mujhe koi chahiye jo ownership le." The word is already in their head — attached to the exact problem they have. Your job is to connect Hire90 to that word.

### The DT Fellowship Angle (use only when relevant)

Hire90 can also place young, AI-native candidates — people in their early 20s who are selected for ownership capability through simulation, not resume. This is the DT Fellowship.

The pitch when it applies: "The 35-year-old you hired last time had the resume but waited for instructions. Our Fellowship candidate is 24, AI-native, picked up the entire pipeline in two weeks. Ownership isn't about years of experience — it's about behaviour. We test for that behaviour before you hire."

**When to use this:** Only if the MD is open to younger talent, or has been burned specifically by experienced hires who didn't deliver. Don't lead with this for MDs who are set on 10+ years experience — handle that objection first, then introduce the Fellowship as an alternative.

## Part 3: The 4 Objections

Record a separate voice note responding to each. Imagine you've just delivered your pitch to Rajesh Mehta, and he says:

---

### Objection 1: "Talk to my HR. They handle all our hiring."

**Why the MD says this:** He's busy. This sounds like a hiring call, and hiring is HR's department in his mental model. He's routing you to where he thinks you belong.

**What he actually means:** He hasn't understood that this is different from regular hiring. He thinks you're a vendor or a recruiter pitching services.

**The real answer lives in:** This isn't a regular hire. This is the hire that HR's process wasn't built for — the one where the last two people failed because nobody defined what ownership actually looks like in this role. This is the MD's hire.

---

### Objection 2: "Just send me some profiles. I'll have a look."

**Why the MD says this:** In his experience, every hiring-related call ends with someone sending CVs. Recruitment firms, staffing companies, LinkedIn — everyone sends profiles. He's giving you the standard response.

**What he actually means:** He thinks you're a recruitment agency. If you agree to send profiles, you've accepted that framing and the conversation is over. You're now one of five firms sending resumes.

**The real answer lives in:** Hire90 doesn't send profiles. Profiles are the last 20% of the problem. The first 80% is: what does this role actually demand, day by day? How do you test whether someone can do it before you hire them? That's what we do. The right profiles come after the role is properly defined.

---

### Objection 3: "We need someone senior for this — 10-15 years experience minimum."

**Why the MD says this:** He equates years of experience with the ability to take ownership. He's been burned by junior people who needed hand-holding. Seniority feels safe.

**What he actually means:** His filter is resume, not behaviour. He hasn't considered that experienced hires also fail at ownership roles — and that's often exactly why he's hiring again.

**The real answer lives in:** Experience tells you what someone has done. Simulation tells you what someone can do. The hire who left after 4 months had experience. The question isn't "how many years" — it's "will this person own the number or wait for instructions." That's what our process tests, regardless of years.

---

### Objection 4: "We already work with [recruitment firm]. They know our industry."

**Why the MD says this:** He has an existing relationship. The firm has placed people before. Switching feels like risk. "They know our industry" is the trust signal.

**What he actually means:** The existing firm sources from the same talent pool using the same method — resume screening and interviews. If the last hire through that firm didn't work out for an ownership role, industry knowledge didn't help. The method is the gap, not the sourcing.

**The real answer lives in:** Your recruitment firm is great for defined roles where the JD is clear and you need someone to fill it. For the BD head role — the one where two people already failed — the problem isn't finding candidates. It's that the standard process can't tell you which candidate will actually take ownership. That's a different problem, and it needs a different process.

---

**For all four objections:** You don't need to demolish the MD's point. You need to keep the conversation alive. The best response acknowledges what the MD said, reframes it, and ends with a reason to keep talking. The goal is "okay, tell me more" — not a signed contract.
