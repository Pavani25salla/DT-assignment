## What This Role Actually Is

Every Community Development person at DeepThought does one thing: **get the right founders into the right conversation rooms.**

You're not selling anything. You're not pitching a product. You're calling an MD and inviting them to sit with 5-8 other founders who are working through the same business problem they are. If it sounds like a sales call, they hang up. If it sounds like someone who genuinely found a conversation they'd enjoy, they listen.

That's the skill this assignment tests.

---

## What You Need to Know About Sciensation

**Sciensation** is DeepThought's community arm. It runs **Scientific Roundtables** every Saturday: 5-8 founders and MDs sit in a room and break down one growth KPI together.

**How it works:**
- One business topic per session (lead generation, conversion, margins, retention, etc.)
- Cross-industry: a pharma manufacturer sits next to a textile exporter. Different businesses, same KPI. The structural pattern underneath surfaces because the industries are different, not despite it
- Socratic method: the moderator asks questions, the room thinks. No presentations. No panels. No speeches
- 90 minutes. Small group. Everyone participates
- The rule: "Interesting wrong answers score higher than correct ones." This means people say what they actually tried and where it broke. That's where the real insight lives

**What it is NOT:**
- Not a conference or webinar (no audience, no stage)
- Not a networking event (nobody is exchanging cards or pitching)
- Not a sales funnel (DeepThought's products are never mentioned in the room)
- Not a peer forum like YPO/Vistage (those run on experience-sharing; this breaks down the structural pattern underneath)

**The dinner test:** If your invitation works, the MD tells someone that evening: "I got a call about this Saturday roundtable. One growth KPI, different industries, Socratic method. A guy from a completely different business had the same structural blocker I did. It's how I think about R&D, applied to my business numbers."

If they'd say "some company called about an event" -- you didn't land it.

### Part A: The Invitation (60%)

Record a voice note. The scenario: you're calling Dr. Venkat Rao. He picked up. Invite him to the upcoming Lead Generation Roundtable.

**Duration:** 60-90 seconds.

**Language:** Hindi, English, or your natural mix -- whatever you'd actually use on a phone call.

**Five things to keep in mind:**

1. **Lead with the topic, not the event.** Don't start with "we're organizing a roundtable." Start with the question the Roundtable is built around. Make him think about his own business before you even mention Sciensation.

2. **Use the dossier.** Connect the topic to Sai Pharma's specific situation. "We're hosting a conversation about lead generation" is generic. Something that references his shift from APIs to formulations is specific.

3. **Position his expertise, not his problem.** You're recognizing what he knows, not diagnosing a gap. "You've built the API business from scratch" works. "Sounds like you're struggling with leads" doesn't.

4. **Talk like a person.** No "exclusive roundtable experience," no "curated peer learning," no "thought leadership platform." If it sounds like a LinkedIn post, don't say it.

5. **End with a low-pressure ask.** "Can I send you the details -- who else is in the room, the exact question, and the date?" is better than "Would you like to register?"

**What we're listening for:** Does this sound like someone genuinely inviting a peer to a conversation, or like someone selling an event ticket? That's the only question. Everything else flows from it.

### Part B: Objection Handling (40%)

After your invitation, Dr. Venkat Rao pushes back. Record **one separate voice note for each** of the 2 objections below.

**Duration:** 30-45 seconds each.

---

**Objection 1: "Is this some kind of sales pitch? What's the catch?"**

He's been burned before. "Founder roundtable" events often turn out to be product demos in disguise. He's protecting his time.

What he actually means: "Prove this is different from the last event that promised peer learning and delivered a sales funnel."

The reality: There is no pitch in the Roundtable. No product demo. No sponsor slot. The conversation belongs to the room.

---

**Objection 2: "I'm very busy this month. Maybe next time."**

Could be genuinely busy. Could be a polite no. "Maybe next time" keeps the door open without committing.

What he actually means: Either he's genuinely stretched, or he's not convinced enough to prioritize this.

Your job: If it feels genuine, respect it and offer to reach out for a future session on a topic that connects to his world. If it feels like a polite deflection, give him one more reason -- not by pushing harder, but by making the topic more specific and relevant.

---

**For both objections:** You're not overcoming resistance. You're helping someone see past their default "no to events" filter and consider whether this specific conversation is different. If they still say no, that's fine.

**What we're listening for:**
- Do you acknowledge what he said, or bulldoze past it?
- Do you stay curious and conversational, or slip into sales mode?
- Do you give him a reason to reconsider, or just push harder?
