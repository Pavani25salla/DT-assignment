## Context: Understanding Amazon Advertising Economics

### The Story

This assignment simulates the actual work you would do on Day 1. The economics, the scenarios, and the client dynamics are drawn from real engagements. If your work is good, it tells us you can do the job, not just answer questions about it.

Imagine you have joined as an AI Business Analyst on an Amazon Ads operations team. Your team manages advertising for brands selling on Amazon India. A brand pays your team a monthly retainer (typically 3% of their ad spend) to manage their Amazon advertising: optimizing bids, selecting keywords, managing campaign budgets, and reporting results.

Your team lead says:

> "We need to understand the economics behind our clients' campaigns. A brand spending ₹15L/month on ads needs to know: is this spend profitable? How much can they afford to spend on acquiring each customer? What happens if their ad efficiency drops? We advise them on these questions every day."

### Why This Matters

Every brand on Amazon is trying to answer the same question: **"Am I making money after advertising costs?"** As an AI Business Analyst, you would help brands answer this question with data, not gut feeling. The model you build here is a simplified version of what you would use daily.

### Key Terms

#### 1. Selling Price (SP)
The price at which the product is listed and sold on Amazon.

**Example:** A skincare brand sells a face wash for ₹500 on Amazon.

#### 2. Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
How much it costs to produce and deliver one unit of the product to Amazon's warehouse.

**Example:** The face wash costs ₹200 to manufacture, package, and ship to Amazon's fulfillment center.

#### 3. Gross Margin
Profit on each unit before any platform fees or advertising costs.

**Formula:** `Gross Margin = SP - COGS`

**Example:** ₹500 - ₹200 = ₹300 gross margin per unit.

#### 4. Amazon Referral Fee
Amazon charges a percentage of the selling price as a fee for every sale made on the platform. This varies by category but is typically 8-15%.

**Formula:** `Amazon Fee = SP × Fee %`

**Example:** ₹500 × 12% = ₹60 per unit.

#### 5. Advertising Cost per Unit
The advertising cost required to generate one sale. If a brand spends ₹50,000 on ads in a month and sells 1,000 units from those ads, the ad cost per unit is ₹50.

**Formula:** `Ad Cost per Unit = Total Ad Spend ÷ Units Sold via Ads`

#### 6. ACOS (Advertising Cost of Sale)
The percentage of ad-attributed revenue spent on advertising.

**Formula:** `ACOS = Ad Spend ÷ Ad Revenue × 100`

**Example:** Spend ₹50,000 on ads, generate ₹2,00,000 in ad-attributed sales → ACOS = 25%.

#### 7. TACOS (Total Advertising Cost of Sale)
The percentage of TOTAL revenue (organic + paid) spent on advertising. This is the health metric because as organic sales grow, TACOS drops even if ad spend stays flat.

**Formula:** `TACOS = Total Ad Spend ÷ Total Revenue (organic + paid) × 100`

#### 8. Net Margin per Unit
Profit per unit after all variable costs.

**Formula:** `Net Margin = Gross Margin - Amazon Fee - Ad Cost per Unit`

#### 9. Monthly Profitability
Total profit from the brand's Amazon business after fixed costs.

**Formula:** `Monthly Profit = (Net Margin × Total Units Sold) - Fixed Costs`

Where fixed costs include: warehousing, team salaries allocated to this brand, software subscriptions, etc.

### Part B: Strategic Conversation, Thinking Beyond the KPI

In your daily work, you would mostly talk to the client's **POC (Point of Contact)**, usually a marketing manager or digital lead. Their world is KPIs: ROAS, ACOS, ad spend efficiency, keyword rankings. These are transactional conversations, important but they only cover "is the campaign performing?"

Above the POC sits a **Director, MD, or someone from the promoter group**. Their world is different. They are thinking about topline revenue, market share, new channels, competitive positioning, and how to grow the overall business, not just one campaign's ROAS.

**Most operations people never learn to see the difference.** They speak the POC's language to everyone. When a Director asks "how are we doing?", they answer with ROAS numbers, and the Director's eyes glaze over because that is not what they asked.

**The growth opportunity lives in the strategic conversation.** When you understand what the MD cares about, you can spot when your operational work reveals a bigger opportunity, and that is where upselling and cross-selling happen naturally.

### The Shift: What Changed Between the Two Conversations

| | POC (Priya, Brand Manager) | MD (Mr. Mehta, Founder) |
|---|---|---|
| **Language** | ROAS, ACOS, keywords, match types, ad creatives | Revenue, growth rate, margin, market share, annualised run rate |
| **Timeframe** | This week → next week | This quarter → next year |
| **Success metric** | Campaign KPIs improving | Topline and bottomline growing |
| **Competitor lens** | "Their product listing images are better" | "They're investing in channels you haven't entered" |
| **Recommendation** | "Let's test Sponsored Display on your top 3 SKUs" | "Let's test Flipkart as your second e-commerce channel, here is the revenue case" |
| **What they do with this info** | Adjusts campaign settings, briefs creative team | Allocates quarterly budget, approves new channel entry, discusses at board level |
| **Your role** | Campaign operator, executing and optimising | Growth advisor, showing where the next crore comes from |

The same person (you) can have both conversations. The same data feeds both, but the lens is completely different. **The Brand Manager thinks in KPIs. The MD thinks in topline and bottomline.** If you speak KPI to the MD, you have lost them. If you speak topline to the Brand Manager, you have confused them.

The analyst who can switch between these two modes, and who knows when each is appropriate, is the one who enables upselling, prevents churn, and earns trust at the level where business decisions are made.

### Why This Matters for You

Most of your daily work is the POC conversation, and that is right. But the analyst who can also see the strategic picture does three things the average ops person cannot:

1. **Spots upsell signals**: "Client's Flipkart store is active but they haven't asked about Flipkart advertising" is a cross-sell opportunity, not just a data point.
2. **Makes effort visible to decision-makers**: the MD never sees the 12 hours/week you invest unless someone translates it into business impact language.
3. **Earns trust at a level that prevents churn**: when the MD sees growth ideas coming from your team, they don't cancel when one month's ROAS is flat.
