## Worked Example: What Good Diagnosis Looks Like

Before you start, here's an example to calibrate the depth we expect. This is a different scenario from the assignment, but it shows the difference between a surface-level reading and a real one.

**The situation:** A manufacturing company rolled out a new ERP system 6 months ago. Adoption is at 30%. Most staff still use the old paper forms. Management says: "People are resistant to change. We need more training."

**Weak diagnosis:** "The employees are not disciplined enough to use the ERP. The company should make ERP usage mandatory, track compliance, and run refresher training sessions until adoption improves."

This restates the stated problem (people are resistant), proposes a compliance fix (make it mandatory), and never asks why people aren't using it.

**Strong diagnosis:** "The ERP requires 15 data fields per entry. The old paper form had 4. Staff experience the ERP as triple the work for zero visible benefit to them, because nobody redesigned the process before digitizing it. They didn't transfer the paper form to the system. They're not resisting the ERP. They're resisting doing 4x the data entry when their metric is production output, not data quality. The real problem: the ERP was built for management reporting, not for the person entering the data. Until the entry experience is redesigned to match the worker's workflow (not the manager's dashboard), adoption will stay at 30% regardless of how many training sessions you run."

Notice what the strong diagnosis does: it looks at the experience from the user's side, identifies the structural mismatch (designed for management, used by operators), and names the behavioral reason (no benefit to the person doing the work). More training won't move the needle here. Redesigning what the user sees will.

**This is the depth we expect in Part A.**

# THE SCENARIO

You're about to read a condensed account of what's happening inside a mid-scale API manufacturing company. This is based on real conversations between a senior advisor and the company's operations team. The information is messy, sometimes contradictory, and not neatly organized, just like reality.

## The Company

PharmaTech Ingredients Pvt. Ltd. (name changed) is an API manufacturer with two manufacturing units, 12 departments, and approximately 200 employees. The company has a CEO (Mr. R), a COO (Mr. B), a General Manager (Mr. S), and 12 department heads (HODs) who manage the shop floor.

Six months ago, the company brought in two young process improvement specialists, Anil and Karthik, from an external consulting firm. Their mandate: introduce systems, improve departmental efficiency, and drive cultural change to make the organization ready for scale.

---

## What Anil and Karthik Have Built

They've done real work:

- **Unified Tracker:** Replaced fragmented department-level Excel sheets with a single unified tracker. The CEO approved it. All 12 managers adopted it. It's still being followed.
- **Automated Daily Alerts:** Built a pipeline: Excel to formulated dashboard to Power Automate to daily email to CEO and COO. Each department's pending action items, RAG status, sent every morning.
- **Meeting Discipline:** Daily status meetings are happening. MOMs are documented. Action items are tracked.
- **Software Attempt:** Tried to implement a project management tool (similar to Monday.com). Failed. The HODs couldn't adapt. Anil and Karthik pivoted to building everything within Excel, which people already used.

---

## What the CEO Says

Mr. R is frustrated. In a recent meeting, he said:

> "I still don't have visibility. I get the daily email. I see red items. But nothing happens. The same items stay red for days. Nobody responds. Nobody escalates. I want to know what's happening on the floor, not what happened yesterday."

In a separate conversation about planning, he was more specific:

> "The production plan is at the heart of everything. If the micro-plan for the day is not clear, nothing downstream works. The alerts, the dashboards, the escalation, all of that is decoration if the base plan for each day doesn't exist."

He's been sharing AI and workflow automation videos with the team, roughly 90+ videos over the past few months. He wants automated workflows, dashboards, and systems thinking. When asked what specifically he wants, he says: "The system should tell me where the problem is before I have to ask."

---

## What Anil and Karthik Say

In a conversation with a senior advisor, they described their typical week:

> **Anil:** "Most of my time goes into follow-ups. I attend the daily meeting, take notes, document action items, then go department to department making sure people do what they committed to. By the time I'm done following up, there's no bandwidth to think about improvements or build new systems."

> **Karthik:** "We were supposed to build systems and drive cultural change. But what we actually do is, if something isn't getting done, we do it ourselves. Yesterday the printer wasn't working. The person responsible was busy. I sat there and got it fixed because if I didn't, it would just stay broken."

> **Anil:** "The managers know their processes. They can do it. But when something unplanned comes up, a machine breakdown, a vendor delay, a sudden client requirement, everything falls apart. They give vague timelines. 'It will be done in 2-3 days.' No micro-plan, no sub-tasks, no dependencies tracked."

> **Karthik:** "We tried implementing PDGMS (a structured daily planning tool). Used it for about 3 months, then stopped. We just restarted it this week."

## What the Senior Advisor Found

A senior advisor conducted two diagnostic sessions with Anil, Karthik, and some department representatives. Here are his observations:

### On the Daily Status Meeting

> "How many people attend this meeting?"
> "Twelve department heads."
> "How many minutes does each person get?"
> "Five to ten minutes."
> "So 12 people times 10 minutes is 120 minutes. Why is this meeting scheduled for 30 minutes?"
> *Silence.*

The meeting was originally scheduled for 1 hour, then compressed to 30 minutes. It still runs 60-90 minutes daily. Technical issues from departments (e.g., out-of-specification lab results) get discussed in this forum instead of being routed to a separate meeting. The output of this meeting generates unplanned work for the rest of the day.

### On the Tracker

> "Show me who is responsible for each task."
> *The tracker shows department names: "Warehouse," "Quality," "Purchase." Not individual names.*
> "If I see 'Warehouse, delayed,' who feels the pressure? Nobody. If I see 'Dinesh, 3 tasks, 1 red,' now Dinesh comes prepared tomorrow."

The tracker has task status (planned date, actual date, RAG color) but no individual accountability. It doesn't show dependencies between tasks or break them into sub-tasks. A complex activity like "Release a Purchase Order" (which involves 5-6 steps across multiple people) appears as a single line item with a single due date.

### On Planning

> "Show me your daily plan for today."
> *Anil pulls up a macro-level weekly plan. No daily micro-plan exists.*
> "So you have a monthly plan and a weekly plan, but no daily plan? Do the department heads have daily plans?"
> "No."
> "Then how do you know what's unplanned? If there's no plan for today, everything is unplanned."

The advisor identified three types of daily activity: routine (scheduled meetings, recurring tasks), planned (project work with defined scope), and unplanned (deviations, breakdowns, ad-hoc demands). At the time of the diagnostic, nobody in the company was categorizing their unplanned work, tracking why unplanned activities happen, or attributing them to a root cause or a responsible person.

### On the Deviation Tracker

The company has a deviation tracking system. When something goes wrong in production (a test fails, a machine behaves unexpectedly, a batch doesn't meet spec), the event is logged and a "root cause" field must be filled.

Here's a sample from one week:

| Date | Department | Deviation | Severity | Root Cause | Corrective Action | Status |
|------|-----------|-----------|----------|------------|-------------------|--------|
| Mon | Quality | OOS result in Batch 247 | High | *blank* | Under investigation | Open |
| Mon | Production | Temperature excursion Unit 2 | Medium | Will be investigated | - | Open |
| Tue | Warehouse | RM received without COA | Low | Vendor issue | Follow up with vendor | Open |
| Wed | Quality | Stability sample failed T=3M | High | *blank* | - | Open |
| Wed | Production | Yield drop 12% vs expected | Medium | Process deviation | *blank* | Open |
| Thu | Maintenance | Reactor agitator seal failure | High | Preventive maintenance not done | Schedule PM | Open |
| Fri | Purchase | Solvent delivery delayed 4 days | Medium | *blank* | - | Open |

70% of root cause fields are either blank or filled with generic text ("will be investigated," "under review," "process deviation"). When the advisor asked Anil and Karthik why, they said:

> "People are not disciplined enough to fill it. We keep reminding them but they don't do it."

When the advisor spoke separately to two supervisors, they said something different:

> **Supervisor 1:** "If I write what actually went wrong, tomorrow in the review meeting they will ask me 50 questions. Why did this happen? Who was responsible? It becomes a blame game. So I leave it blank and handle it quietly."

> **Supervisor 2:** "Last time I wrote the real root cause, that the maintenance schedule was not followed because we didn't have the spare part, the maintenance head got called out in front of everyone. After that, nobody writes root cause honestly."
